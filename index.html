This page gives an account of my learning journey prior to entering LaunchCode.

Saint Louis University

My BA institution

I attended Saint Louis University (SLU) from 1994-1998. I entered as a Physical Therapy major. SLU's liberal arts curriculum required me to take several Philosophy classes. I really enjoyed the amount and kind of thinking required in Philosophy classes, and decided to get a philosophy minor. But during my junior year, I became disallusioned with many of the Physical Therapy classes. They emphasized rote memorization, with hardly any critical or creative thinking. I decided break from physical therapy and to cram the classes necessary for a philosophy degree into my senior year. After doing that, I was encouraged to apply to graduate school by the Philosophy Department Chair. I did and got into the University of Cincinnati's MA program. I had applied to more than a dozen schools, but UC was the only one that accepted me!

The University of Cincinnati

My MA institution

I entered UC in 1998 and graduated in 2001. Based on my undergraduate training, I thought I would concentrate in either ancient philosophy or ethics. To really work on ancient philosophy would have required learning Ancient Greek, which was a bit of a barrier. Additionally, I was captivated by the idea that if we figured out what justice required, everyone might agree about what we need to do to make our society more just. Unfortuantely, I hadn't taken any political philosophy courses as an undergraduate, so there was a lot of catching up to do! I was learning a lot more about how to better argue and about recent work in political philosophy. As it turned out, though, the paper that I used to apply to PhD programs was on Paul Feyerabend's work on incommensurability, the idea that new scientific theories may use concepts and methods that represent such a break from older scientific theories that the transition between the two may not be fully rational. This time, I was a much better prepared student and I got into all three schools I applied to, ultimately deciding on The Ohio State University.

The Ohio State University

My PhD institution

I didn't continue working directly on philosophy of science or political philosophy at OSU. Instead, I ended up focusing on moral responsibility: the concept connected to blaming, praising, punishing, and rewarding other people. Roughly, the idea is that it doesn't seem appropriate to blame, punish, etc someone unless she is morally responsible for what she's done. That much seems (relatively) clear. But there are lots of puzzles and difficulties! The one that I focused on in my disseration is what's known as the problem of manipulation. Suppose that nefarious neuroscientists were able to control someone's brain in a very subtle way, so that they could make that person do whatever the neuroscientists wanted. But at the same time, the person would want to do whatever the neuroscientists wanted! Suppose the neuroscientists want to make Zac insult Joe. Well, then it will occur to Zac that insulting Joe is an awesome idea and Zac will insult Joe on purpose. Zac will probably even then feel satisfied with himself!

What should we make of this situation? Well, you might think that because of what the neuroscientists did, Zac isn't morally responsible for insulting Joe. So Joe shouldn't blame Zac. But if that's right, we should ask why Zac isn't morally responsible for what he did. Is it because his actions were caused by factors (what the neuroscientits did) that were out of his control? Well, if that's right, then maybe none of us are ever morally responsible for what we did! Because all of our actions are ultimately caused by other things (our genes, upbringing, what other people do, etc.) that aren't in our control.

People that agree with this reasoning are hard determinists: they think that if all of our actions are really determined by things out of our control then none of us can ever be morally responsible for what we do. I actually think this is wrong and, in my disseration, I tried to explain why. Very quickly, my idea is that we think it would be unfair to blame Zac in this situation because there's anohter party --the Neuroscientists--who it seems we should blame instead. Because the Neuroscientists intentionally caused Zac to do what he did, we should blame them! But in almost every regular case, no one is intentionally messing with our brains to make us do what we do.

Anyway, that got kind of longwinded! Sorry about that. (I'd be happy to chat more, if you're interested.) The important bit is that I finished my PhD in 2010 and was hired at Northern Michigan University.

Northern Michigan University

My current employer

I earned tenure in 2014. I really enjoy my work, but I don't enjoy how much of it there is. We teach approximately double the amount that professors at larger, better funded school do. That means that I either have to accept being continually behind in research, or work too hard. Additionally, while the setting of NMU is lovely (right on the shores of Lake Superior), the winter lasts about six months and we're 5 hours from the nearest major city, Milwaukee. I'm on sabbatical this year and my partner and I came to St. Louis to work and get rejuvenated. (We have lots of friends here from my college days.) I can't remember how, but we discovered LaunchCode. And, actually, we're both doing it. She starts next week!
